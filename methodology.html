Technical Methodology

The Toxic Sites Identification Program combines two complementary machine learning models to detect informal lead contamination sources across South Asia.

Two Complementary Pipelines

Our screening approach integrates contextual analysis with remote sensing to maximize geographic coverage while maintaining detection accuracy. The two models operate in parallel and can be deployed independently based on data availability and regional priorities.

Both pipelines output a risk score (0.0-1.0) and confidence metrics that feed into the final prioritization engine.

Contextual Model: ULAB Detection

Overview

The Contextual Model identifies informal lead-acid battery (ULAB) recycling operations using an XGBoost classifier trained on geospatial, socioeconomic, and environmental features. ULAB sites are characterized by clustering of battery shops, scrap dealers, and informal settlements without environmental controls.

Algorithm

XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting) is a gradient boosting decision tree ensemble that excels at capturing non-linear relationships in structured data. The model is trained to output a probability score for each grid cell (1km²) indicating the likelihood of ULAB activity.

Model: XGBoost
Input: 2,500+ labeled sites with 40+ geospatial features
Output: Risk score 0.0-1.0 + uncertainty bounds
Training regions: India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Cambodia

Feature Engineering

The model incorporates the following feature categories:

  • ◆

    Business Clustering

    Density of battery shops, scrap metal dealers, and informal recycling businesses within 500m radius. Derived from OpenStreetMap POI data and Google Places API.

  • ◆

    Population Demographics

    Population density, informal settlement presence, presence of educational institutions (schools, hospitals). Sources: WorldPop, settlement layers from OpenStreetMap.

  • ◆

    Environmental Indicators

    Proximity to water sources, heavy vehicle traffic patterns, air quality proxies, soil contamination estimates. Derived from traffic data, water datasets, and remote sensing.

  • ◆

    Regulatory Environment

    Industrial zoning, informal settlement classification, distance to regulated waste facilities. Source: administrative boundaries and OSM zoning layers.

  • ◆

    Historical Data

    Previous TSIP field visit results, community reports, NGO records of contamination incidents. Aggregated at grid level with temporal decay.

Metric Value Interpretation

Satellite Imagery Model: Smelter Detection

Overview

The Satellite Model detects industrial smelter facilities using a convolutional neural network (CNN) trained on Sentinel-2 multispectral satellite imagery. Smelters have distinctive thermal, spectral, and structural signatures that can be detected even when not registered in official records.

Algorithm

ResNet-50 CNN is a 50-layer residual network that learns hierarchical features from image patches (128×128 pixels, 10m resolution). The model outputs a probability score indicating the likelihood of smelter activity.

Model: ResNet-50 (CNN)
Input: Sentinel-2 12-band multispectral imagery
Resolution: 10m per pixel
Spectral bands: Coastal, Blue, Green, Red, SWIR, NDVI
Output: Risk score 0.0-1.0 + confidence bounds

Feature Space

The CNN automatically learns the following latent features from raw imagery:

  • ◆

    Thermal Signatures

    Smelters emit heat; this is detected through SWIR (Short-Wave Infrared) band anomalies. Active smelting shows distinctive thermal patterns distinct from normal industrial facilities.

  • ◆

    Spectral Characteristics

    Lead and other heavy metals create distinctive spectral reflectance patterns. The model learns to recognize contaminated soils and dust surrounding smelters.

  • ◆

    Building Morphology

    Smelters have distinctive architectural patterns: large roof areas, multiple chimneys, industrial layout. CNN learns these structural signatures.

  • ◆

    Emission Patterns

    Smoke and dust plumes from smelters create visible signatures. Temporal analysis of multiple acquisitions reveals sustained activity patterns.

  • ◆

    Vegetation Index

    NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) shows reduced vegetation near contaminated smelter sites due to air pollution and heavy metal accumulation.

Training Data

The model is trained on a curated dataset combining:

  • 500+ confirmed smelter locations from USGS industrial facility database
  • ILZSG (International Lead and Zinc Study Group) smelter registries
  • Pure Earth TSIP field-verified sites
  • Sentinel-2 archive from 2019-2026 covering South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and Southeast Asia
  • Negative samples: legitimate industrial facilities (chemical plants, refineries) and urban areas

Active Learning Loop

The TSIP program implements a continuous improvement cycle where field verification results feed back into model refinement. This approach ensures the models remain calibrated to ground truth as conditions change.

Process

  1. Prediction: Models run monthly or quarterly on new satellite data and POI updates
  2. Prioritization: High-confidence detections are sorted by risk score and clustered geographically
  3. Field Verification: Pure Earth and partner teams visit high-priority sites and collect structured field data
  4. Data Integration: Field observations (site type, activity level, workforce size, environmental controls) are logged
  5. Model Evaluation: Predictions are compared against field results; false positives and false negatives are analyzed
  6. Retraining: Models are retrained quarterly on accumulated ground truth data
  7. Calibration: Model hyperparameters and feature weights are adjusted based on regional performance

Limitations & Assumptions

Known Limitations

  • Cloud cover: Satellite-based detection is limited by cloud cover in monsoon regions. Temporal compositing helps but cannot eliminate seasonal gaps.
  • Small facilities: Sites with rooftops <100m² may be difficult to detect reliably. The model is optimized for medium to large operations.
  • Regional variation: Model performance varies by region due to differences in urban density, building materials, and climate. Contextualization by region is recommended.
  • Data freshness: Satellite imagery lags by 5-12 days. POI data from OpenStreetMap may be outdated in rapidly changing informal settlements.
  • Informal sector dynamics: ULAB sites frequently relocate. A negative prediction does not guarantee absence of activity; follow-up is important.
  • False positives: Some industrial facilities (e.g., battery manufacturers) may be mistaken for informal ULAB sites. Field teams must differentiate.

Methodological Assumptions

  • ULAB sites concentrate in areas with high informal business density
  • Smelter operations produce detectable thermal and spectral signatures
  • POI data from OpenStreetMap and Google Places has reasonable coverage in South Asia
  • Lead contamination patterns correlate with site locations
  • Field verification teams can reliably classify site types and activity levels

Data Sources & Attribution

Geospatial Data

  • OpenStreetMap (OSM): POI data, building footprints, road networks, administrative boundaries
  • Google Places API: Business listings, shop locations, amenity data
  • WorldPop: Population density grids at 100m resolution
  • ESA Copernicus: Sentinel-2 multispectral satellite imagery
  • NASA USGS: Industrial facility database, historical smelter records

Validation Data

  • Pure Earth TSIP: 2,500+ field-verified toxic sites across South Asia
  • ILZSG: International Lead and Zinc Study Group smelter registry
  • USGS: Geological Survey industrial facility records
  • Academic literature: Published studies on lead contamination hotspots

Data Citation: Users of this model are encouraged to cite the underlying data sources and acknowledge the Pure Earth TSIP program. Raw output predictions should not be used as the sole basis for remediation decisions without field verification.
